## What Is the Alternative Minimum Tax?

The Alternative Minimum Tax (AMT) is a parallel federal income tax calculation that runs alongside the regular income tax system. It was created in 1969 after Congress discovered that 155 high-income taxpayers paid zero federal income tax through heavy use of tax preferences and deductions.

The AMT ensures that high earners pay **at least a minimum amount** of tax, regardless of how many deductions and preferences they claim.

## How AMT Works

You calculate your taxes **twice** — once under the regular tax system and once under AMT rules — and pay whichever is higher.

**AMT Calculation:**
1. Start with regular taxable income
2. Add back AMT "preference items" and "adjustments" (items disallowed under AMT)
3. Subtract the AMT exemption amount
4. Apply the AMT rates: **26%** on the first $232,600 / **28%** above that (2024)

## 2024 AMT Exemptions

The AMT exemption shields most of your income from the AMT:

| Filing Status | Exemption | Phase-Out Begins |
|---|---|---|
| Single | $85,700 | $609,350 |
| Married Filing Jointly | $133,300 | $1,218,700 |
| Married Filing Separately | $66,650 | $609,350 |

Because the exemption phases out for high earners, the effective AMT rate can reach as high as 35% in the phase-out range.

## Common AMT "Add-Backs"

These items are allowed under regular tax but **disallowed or limited under AMT**:

- **State and local tax (SALT) deduction** — the $10,000 SALT deduction is entirely disallowed under AMT
- **Standard deduction** — not allowed under AMT
- **Incentive Stock Options (ISOs)** — the spread at exercise is an AMT preference item
- **Accelerated depreciation** — must use straight-line under AMT
- **Percentage depletion** — oil and gas depletion above cost basis
- **Passive activity losses** — certain limitations differ

## Who Is Subject to AMT?

After the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act (2017) dramatically increased exemptions and phase-out thresholds, far fewer people are affected. You're most at risk if you:

- Exercise Incentive Stock Options (ISOs) in a year when the stock price is high
- Have very high SALT taxes (especially in California, New York, New Jersey)
- Have significant miscellaneous itemized deductions
- Have large accelerated depreciation deductions
- Have substantial passive income adjustments

In 2024, fewer than **200,000 taxpayers** are expected to owe AMT — down from over 4 million before TCJA.

## The AMT Credit

If you paid AMT in a prior year because of **deferral preferences** (like ISO exercises), you may be entitled to a **Minimum Tax Credit (MTC)** in future years when your regular tax exceeds your tentative minimum tax. This credit prevents permanent double taxation.

## ISO Exercises and AMT: A Critical Warning

Exercising Incentive Stock Options (ISOs) is the most common AMT trigger for employees at tech companies and startups. The **bargain element** (fair market value minus exercise price) is not taxable under regular income tax at exercise — but **is an AMT preference item**.

If you exercise ISOs when the stock is significantly above the strike price, you could owe substantial AMT even with no cash proceeds. This has caught many tech employees off guard.

**Strategy**: Model the AMT before exercising ISOs, especially in a year with high stock prices. Consider spreading ISO exercises across multiple tax years.

## How to Check If You Owe AMT

IRS Form 6251 (Alternative Minimum Tax — Individuals) is used to calculate whether you owe AMT. Your tax software completes this automatically. If tentative minimum tax on Form 6251 exceeds your regular tax, you owe the difference as AMT.

## Strategies to Reduce AMT Exposure

- **Timing ISO exercises carefully** — exercise when stock price is low or in a low-income year
- **Avoiding large SALT itemized deductions** (already largely neutralized by the $10k cap)
- **Accelerating income into AMT years** — if you'll pay AMT anyway, recognize more income in that year
- **Deferring preference items** — spread deductions over multiple years

---

*AMT rules are complex and the calculations depend heavily on your specific situation. Consult a CPA or tax attorney if you're subject to ISO exercises or other common AMT triggers.*
